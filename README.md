<h1>Actions Against 3D Printing of Guns or Gun Parts</h1>
<p><strong>As of May 2026</strong>, here is a comprehensive list of companies, organizations, and individuals that have taken verifiable public actions against 3D printing gun rights and access.</p>

<h2>1. 3D Model Hosting &amp; Design Platforms</h2>
<ul>
    <li><strong>Thingiverse</strong> (Makerspace / largest public repository)<br>
        Deployed AI/automated scanning to detect and remove 3D-printed firearm and gun part files after contact from Manhattan DA (July 2025). Strengthened enforcement of existing weapons ban in TOS.</li>
</ul>

<h2>2. 3D Printing Service Bureaus</h2>
<ul>
    <li><strong>Sculpteo</strong> (France) : Explicit policy refusing to print any guns, gun parts, or weapon-related items (in place since 2018).</li>
    <li><strong>Shapeways</strong> : Long-standing content policy prohibiting guns, realistic replicas, magazines, and related items.</li>
</ul>

<h2>3. 3D Printing Software &amp; Workflow Companies</h2>
<ul>
    <li><strong>Print&amp;Go</strong> (Spain) : Released <strong>3D GUN’T</strong> (2024), an AI + database system that scans and blocks firearm components in slicer/cloud workflows.</li>
    <li><strong>3DPrinterOS</strong> : Collaborating on AI detection/blocking algorithms (with Montclair State University) for gun parts in print jobs.</li>
</ul>

<h2>4. Individuals &amp; Law Enforcement</h2>
<ul>
    <li><strong>Manhattan District Attorney Alvin Bragg</strong><br>
        Most active enforcer:<br>
        • Sent letters to Creality and other manufacturers urging detection software installation.<br>
        • Secured Thingiverse’s AI scanning agreement.<br>
        • Public advocacy and data releases on ghost guns.</li>
</ul>

<h2>5. Advocacy Organizations</h2>
<ul>
    <li><strong>Everytown for Gun Safety</strong> (and Moms Demand Action) : Published reports and lobbied for mandatory detection tech and file removal.</li>
</ul>
